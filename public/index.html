Welcome to DiscoGraph, a simple interactive data-visualization built with d3 and the Discogs API.

To use the graph:

  • Adjust the sliders to the years you're interested in viewing (between 1950 and 2016)
  • Click the buttons to display or hide a particular genre
  • Use the style field to look for a style* you don't see
You can find all kind of cool information this way.
  • Did you know that in 1978 there were more releases in the funk/soul genre than pop? I didn't!
  • Also, check out the trend lines for Funk/Soul and Disco. Shockingly similar!

* We rely on the Discogs API where many things you or I might call a genre are classified as a style, for example punk or disco.

Music is swell! But, as glamorous as practicing hours on end in a hot basement or tweaking the same beat for a month straight is, it's also a lot of work. A typical album costs ~ $10 and runs about an hour long. If you listen to it twice, you've gotten about as much direct engagement as a hollywood movie for a little bit less than the price of a ticket.

By its own admission Spotify pays 'rights holders' (i.e. often not artists), on average, less than a penny (btw $.006 and $.0084) per stream per song. To get to that $10 mark, you'd have to stream a song for 70 hours. I'm not picking on Spotify either, they are just the most open with their rates.

Imagine how much you would hate sitting through a 70 hour movie.

Put differently, if you stream music for 1 hour every single day of the year, you've gotten money to rights-holders for about 5 albums a year.

This isn't to put streaming down. I stream all the time, and I love it. Companies that provide this service need to make ends meet as well. But, you can stream an album even after you've bought it.
